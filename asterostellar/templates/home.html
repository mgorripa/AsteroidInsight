Distance from the Sun

Asteroids, those nomadic remnants of our solar system's formation, embark on a celestial journey that brings them closest and farthest from the radiant embrace of the Sun

Nearest to the Sun (Perihelion):

When asteroids reach their perihelion, they are at their closest point to the Sun in their elliptical orbits. This proximity brings them to searing temperatures and intense solar radiation. Perihelion distances can vary greatly depending on the asteroid's orbit, but generally, asteroids can get as close as a few million kilometers from the Sun's surface. Some asteroids with highly eccentric orbits, like members of the Atira or Amor groups, can even have perihelion distances closer than Mercury's average distance from the Sun

Farthest from the Sun (Aphelion)

Conversely, at aphelion, asteroids reach their farthest point from the Sun in their orbits. This distance can vary significantly depending on the asteroid's orbital eccentricity. For asteroids within the main asteroid belt, aphelion distances typically range from about 2 to 3 astronomical units (AU), with 1 AU being the average distance from the Earth to the Sun. However, certain asteroids with highly eccentric orbits, such as those in the Jupiter-family or Halley-type groups, can have aphelion distances stretching well beyond the orbit of Jupiter, sometimes even extending into the outer reaches of the solar system.
